RAG Playground · Use case 01 · Unstructured data

Turn the knobs. Watch retrieval decide the answer.

Everything below really runs: the clauses are embedded in your browser, scored by cosine similarity, filtered, re-ranked, and assembled into a prompt. The answer only ever uses what retrieval put on the desk — so break retrieval and watch the answer break with it.

How this works — read before you touch the knobs 6 steps

You are an insurance claims adjuster's co-pilot. Your knowledge base is 14 real clauses pulled from five systems: two policy wordings (the current H-2019 and the retired G-2018), a claims handbook, a prior settled decision, and one adjuster email. A question comes in. Six things happen between the question and the answer — and you control four of them.

STEP 01 · OFFLINE

Collect the sources

Connectors read every system on a schedule and drop the raw content into one pile, keeping filename, version and date intact. This step is already done for you — the 14 clauses are the pile.

The lessonMiss a source here and the model is permanently blind to it. Coverage of the input is the first ceiling on answer quality.

STEP 02 · OFFLINE · YOURS

Cut into chunks, tag each one

A 180-page policy is useless to a model whole. The chunker splits it and stamps each piece with metadata: policy variant, date, source. Use the Chunking control to choose how the cut is made.

Watch thisSection-aware gives one clause per chunk, whole. Fixed 180 chars slices blindly — a chunk can score well and still not carry the fact you needed.

STEP 03 · OFFLINE

Embed into meaning space

Each chunk becomes a vector — a list of numbers standing for its meaning. Clauses about the same idea land near each other even when the words differ, because wind and storm map to the same concept.

Watch thisThe scatter plot is that space, flattened to 2-D. Distance is real: it comes from the actual vectors, recomputed on every keystroke.

STEP 04 · LIVE · YOURS

The adjuster asks

Pick a preset question or type your own. The system quietly attaches the claim's context — policy variant, endorsement, jurisdiction — and embeds the question with the same model that embedded the chunks, so "near" means something.

Watch thisThe black diamond is your question. Everything near it is what the system thinks is relevant, before any rule is applied.

STEP 05 · LIVE · YOURS

Retrieve, filter, re-rank

Stage one grabs the nearest k candidates — fast, rough, wide. Filters then drop anything from the wrong policy or a superseded version. The re-ranker re-reads what survives and reorders it. Top 3 go forward.

Watch thisStruck-through rows were dropped, and the tag says why. Filters fire here — never after the model has spoken, so it can't leak what it shouldn't see.

STEP 06 · LIVE

Generate with citations

The 3 surviving passages, the question, and one strict instruction — answer only from these, cite everything, admit it when you can't — go to the model. The prompt panel shows the exact text. Nothing else reaches the model.

The lessonThe model is constrained, not free. If the answer isn't in those 3 passages, it says so. That constraint is the cure for hallucination.

Four experiments — run them in order

  1. Admit the wrong policy. Keep the first question. Turn Filter to policy H-2019 off. G-2018 § 4.2 walks into the prompt: same words, same date, £5,000 instead of £25,000. It isn't out of date — it is a different customer's policy, and its vector is almost identical to the right clause. Similarity cannot tell them apart. Metadata can. The verdict drops to confidence low and the answer names the contradiction instead of averaging it away.
  2. Admit a stale precedent. Filters back on, then turn Filter to current versions off. Prior decision C-31877 arrives — right policy, real settlement, £15,000 — from 2022, before P-441 existed. It answers a question that is no longer live. Two filters, two entirely different failures: one is wrong, one is expired.
  3. Break the chunker. All guards on. Switch chunking to Fixed 180 chars. Scores still look healthy — but the clause was cut mid-sentence and the fact went missing. The model refuses. Compare the honesty of this failure with a system that would have guessed a number.
  4. Watch a knob do nothing. Pick question 2 and toggle the re-ranker. The third passage swaps — Handbook for § 5 Excess — and the answer doesn't move. At 14 clauses with distinctive vocabulary, plain vector search already wins. Re-rankers earn their latency at 280,000 clauses, where fifty chunks all say "detached structures are covered." Not every box in a reference architecture is doing work in your corpus. Measure before you pay for it.

How to read the answer panel — three states, not two. Grounded · confidence high: every clause needed to answer correctly reached the prompt, and nothing contradicts it. Grounded · confidence low: the answer is right, but something in the context disputes it — a rival policy or an expired precedent — and the panel says which. Unsupported: retrieval failed, the model declined, and the panel names the clause that never arrived. When you type your own question there is no known-good answer to check against, so the model just reports what it retrieved — judge it yourself against the passages.
