Every kind of attention, made tangible

Attention is the trick that lets AI focus on the right words. Read "the trophy didn't fit in the suitcase because it was too big" — you instantly know "it" means the trophy. Attention is how a model does that. It comes in a few flavors; these six hands-on tools let you play with each one, in plain language.

Tip: start at "The core" — every other type is a twist on it.
01 · The mechanism

Scaled dot-product attention

Every kind of attention works the same way — like looking something up in a library: compare what you want against every book, turn the matches into percentages of your focus, then pull out a blend of the most relevant ones. Click a word to make it the one doing the looking.

How it works, step by step
  1. Three roles. Each word plays three parts: what it's looking for, a label advertising what it offers, and the information it hands over. Think library search: your topic, the labels on the book spines, and the contents inside. (Their technical names are Query, Key, and Value.)
  2. Compare. The word doing the looking checks against every other word's label to see how well they match.
  3. Keep it sensible. Big matches get toned down a bit, so one loud match doesn't drown out everything else.
  4. Turn into percentages. The matches become a split of your focus that adds up to 100% — maybe 70% one word, 20% another.
  5. Blend. The result is a mix of the words' information, weighted by those percentages. Example: for "it" in "the animal didn't cross… because it was tired," most of the focus lands on "animal," so "it" quietly picks up the meaning of "animal."
02 · The scoring function

How the score is computed: additive vs dot-product

How it works, step by step
  1. Quick score (dot-product). A fast similarity rating between two words — like glancing at two dating profiles and rating how alike they are. Easy, but the scores balloon as the word descriptions get longer.
  2. Toned-down score (scaled). The same quick score, divided down so it stays sensible no matter how long the descriptions are. Slide the length up: the plain version lurches to all-or-nothing while this one stays calm. Every modern model uses this.
  3. Careful score (additive). A tiny built-in judge weighs the two words together. Slower, but it works even when the two things being compared aren't the same size.
  4. Same next step. However you score the match, the scores then become percentages of focus — so this choice only changes how "match" is measured, not what happens next.
03 · Where the Q/K/V come from

Self-attention vs cross-attention

Same idea, two wirings. In self-attention, words in one sentence look at each other. In cross-attention, the words being written look back at a different text — like a translator glancing at the original.

Self-attention

How it works, step by step
  1. Self-attention. Every word looks at the other words in its own sentence — how a model works out that "it" means "the animal." Like a group in one room, each person listening to everyone else in that same room.
  2. Cross-attention. The text being produced looks back at a different source text. A translator writing French keeps glancing at the English to line each word up. This powers translation, summarizing, and photo captioning.
  3. Why it matters. Understanding-only models (BERT) use just self-attention; text generators (GPT) use self-attention that can only look backward; translators use both, plus cross-attention to connect the output to the input.
04 · Who can attend to whom

Attention patterns: global, local, causal & sparse

Letting every word look at every other is powerful but slow — for a long document it's like making everyone at a huge conference meet everyone else. Restricting who looks at whom is how models read long things. Explore the common shapes.

Pattern

lit cell = query (row) may attend to key (column) · fewer lit cells = less compute
How it works, step by step
  1. Full. Every word looks at all the others — the most thorough, but the work grows fast (double the length, quadruple the work), so it can't handle very long text.
  2. Causal. A word may only look backward at earlier words — needed to write text one word at a time (like autocomplete).
  3. Sliding window. Each word only checks its nearby neighbors — cheap, and information still spreads as it passes through layers, like gossip crossing a room. Used by long-document models.
  4. Dilated / block / global+local. Skip around, group words into chunks, or keep a few "announcement" words everyone hears. These shortcuts read book-length text at a fraction of the cost — the density number shows how much work you save.
05 · How much to commit

Soft vs hard attention

How it works, step by step
  1. Soft (dimmer). Focus is split across everything by percentages, and the result is a blend. Because it's smooth, the model learns easily — which is why nearly every real system uses it.
  2. Hard (on/off). Pick exactly one word to look at. Cheaper, and easy to explain since you can point to the one thing it looked at — but "pick one" is an abrupt choice that's hard to learn from, so it stays mostly a research idea.
  3. The dial between them. Turning the focus up makes soft attention sharper until it almost picks one thing; turning it down spreads focus evenly.
06 · Many views & the KV-cache trick

Multi-head — and MQA / GQA

One focuser notices one kind of link. Running a team of them in parallel — each spotting something different — is multi-head attention. Modern models also make them share notes to go faster.

How it works, step by step
  1. Multi-head. Run several focusers at once, each trained to notice a different thing — nearby grammar, who "it" refers to, verb-and-object pairs. Then combine what they each found. Like a grammar expert, a character-tracker, and an action-tracker all reading the same sentence.
  2. The catch. As the model writes, each focuser keeps notes on everything so far. Many focusers means a huge pile of notes — which slows generation down.
  3. MQA — one shared notebook. All focusers share a single set of notes: much lighter and faster, with a small quality cost.
  4. GQA — a few shared notebooks. Focusers share in small groups: almost the full quality with most of the speed. Used by Llama and other popular models.
