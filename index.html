The Lineage

Seven ideas, each answering the last

Follow the thread top to bottom. The rose note on each card is the specific limitation that made the next idea necessary.

The premise

What a language model is

assign probability to sequences

A language model scores how likely a sequence of words is, which lets it predict the next token. Factor the joint probability with the chain rule and the whole field becomes one question: how do you estimate each conditional term?

P(w₁…wₙ) = ∏ P(wᵢ | w₁…wᵢ₋₁)
The catch

Conditioning on the entire history is impossible to estimate directly — the contexts are never seen twice.

1990s · Counting

N-gram models

the Markov shortcut

Approximate the full history with the last n−1 words, then just count. A bigram model asks only "given the previous word, what comes next?" — estimated from corpus frequencies with smoothing to survive unseen pairs.

P(wᵢ | wᵢ₋ₙ₊₁ … wᵢ₋₁)
Breaks on

Counts get sparse fast, the model forgets anything beyond a few words back, and every word is an atomic symbol with no notion of similarity.

2013 · Meaning as geometry

Word embeddings

Word2Vec · GloVe

Replace one-hot symbols with dense vectors learned so that words used in similar contexts land near each other. Word2Vec learns them by predicting context (Skip-gram / CBOW); GloVe factorizes a global co-occurrence matrix. Suddenly king − man + woman ≈ queen.

Breaks on

Each word gets one fixed vector regardless of sentence, and embeddings alone don't model word order — you still need something to read a sequence.

Lab 1 · train & explore embeddings
2010s · Reading in order

Recurrent networks (RNNs)

a hidden state that carries the past

Process tokens one at a time, folding each new input into a hidden state that acts as memory. The same weights apply at every step, so in principle an RNN can use context of any length.

hₜ = tanh(Wₓ xₜ + Wₕ hₜ₋₁ + b)
Breaks on

Gradients vanish across many steps, so "in principle unlimited" memory becomes "in practice a handful of words." Training is also strictly sequential.

Lab 2 · a char-level RNN
Gated memory

LSTMs & their limits

forget · input · output gates

LSTMs add a protected cell state and learnable gates that decide what to keep, add, and expose. The gating lets gradients flow across far longer spans, which is why LSTMs powered a decade of translation and speech.

Breaks on

Still fundamentally sequential (no parallelism over time), still strained by very long dependencies, and the whole past is squeezed through one fixed-size state.

Lab 2 · RNN vs LSTM, side by side
2015 · Look anywhere

The attention mechanism

query · key · value

Instead of forcing everything through one state, let each position look directly at every other position and pull in what's relevant. Compare a query to all keys, softmax into weights, take a weighted sum of values — dependencies become distance-independent.

Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QKᵀ/√dₖ) V
The opening

If attention alone can relate any two tokens in one step, do we even need recurrence?

Lab 3 · attention from scratch
2017 · Attention is all you need

The Transformer

stacked self-attention + feed-forward

Drop recurrence entirely. Multi-head self-attention mixes information across the sequence in parallel; positional encodings restore word order; residual connections, layer norm, and feed-forward blocks stack into deep encoders and decoders. Fully parallel, scales beautifully — the architecture behind modern LLMs.

The payoff

Every earlier limitation — sparsity, forgetting, no parallelism — answered at once.
